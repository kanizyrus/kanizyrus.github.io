For five years, I made the same mistake. Someone on my team would bring me a problem, and I would solve it. Quickly, efficiently, with the satisfaction of a senior engineer who still had it. I told myself I was being helpful. I was actually being destructive.

## The Pattern

It usually went like this: a developer would come to me with a tricky bug, a design question, or an architectural decision they weren't sure about. I'd listen for about two minutes, then jump in with the answer. Sometimes I'd even take over their keyboard.

The code got fixed. The architecture got designed. And slowly, without anyone noticing, I created a team that couldn't make decisions without me.

## The Breaking Point

The breaking point came during a week I was sick. Nothing dramatic — just a bad flu that kept me away from my laptop for five days. When I came back, I found three pull requests that had been waiting for my review, two architectural decisions that were "on hold until Ping is back," and a bug that a junior developer had actually fixed on day one but hadn't merged because they wanted my approval first.

I had become a bottleneck. Worse, I had taught my team to be dependent on me.

## What I Changed

The shift was simple to describe and painful to execute: I stopped giving answers and started asking questions.

When someone brought me a problem, instead of solving it, I'd ask: "What have you tried so far?" and "What do you think the right approach is?" Then I'd shut up and wait. The silence was excruciating at first.

90% of the time, they already had a good solution. They just didn't trust it yet because I had spent years training them not to trust themselves.

## The Multiplier Test

I started applying what I call the Multiplier Test to every task: "Will doing this myself multiply the team's capability, or just add my individual output?" If it's the latter, I delegate. Even if I could do it faster. Especially if I could do it faster.

The hardest part wasn't changing my behavior. It was sitting with the discomfort of watching someone take three hours on something I could have done in thirty minutes — and recognizing that those three hours were an investment, not a waste.

## Five Years Later

My team now makes architectural decisions without me in the room. They ship features while I'm on vacation. A junior developer I mentored just gave a conference talk about a system she designed entirely on her own.

That's what leadership looks like when you stop being the hero. It's slower, quieter, and infinitely more valuable than anything I could build alone.
