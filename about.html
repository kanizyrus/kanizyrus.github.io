About me

My name is Ping-Hsien Lin, and most people call me Kaniz. I'm a Taiwanese software engineer who has been living in Bangkok, Thailand since 2012. I studied Computer Science at National Taiwan University, and I've spent the years since then building software, starting a company, leading engineering teams, and trying to understand how technology actually helps people — as opposed to how we like to imagine it does.

Starting close to the metal

My first jobs were in embedded systems and ERP consulting. I spent years working with business intelligence platforms and enterprise resource planning systems — the kind of software that isn't glamorous but runs real companies. It was during this work that I started noticing a pattern that would eventually change the direction of my career.

Small businesses struggled. Not with technology in the abstract, but with understanding their own financial situation. They couldn't easily see where their money was going, couldn't manage their financial data effectively, and couldn't access the financing and resources they needed to grow. The tools that existed were either too complex, too expensive, or built for companies ten times their size.

I kept thinking: someone should fix this. Eventually, I realized that someone might have to be me.

Building Pymlo

That's how Pymlo started. Not as a "fintech startup" with a pitch deck and a growth strategy, but as a cloud-first accounting platform born from years of watching small businesses struggle with problems that better software could solve.

We built the platform from scratch. We navigated Thai financial regulations. We learned, slowly and sometimes painfully, what it means to handle other people's money — not as an abstraction, but as a daily responsibility.

Pymlo taught me things that no amount of reading could have. It taught me that trust isn't a feature you add — it's the foundation you build everything on. It taught me that regulated technology demands a discipline that startup culture actively discourages: patience, caution, and the willingness to move slowly when moving slowly is the right thing to do. And it taught me that the gap between building something technically impressive and building something genuinely useful is wider than most engineers think.

The company didn't become a unicorn. But the lessons from those years are in everything I do now.

From building software to building teams

After Pymlo, I moved into engineering leadership in a global enterprise. Today I lead a chapter of full-stack developers — which means my job has shifted from writing code to creating the conditions where other people write better code.

That transition was harder than I expected. For years, my identity was tied to being the person who could solve the hardest technical problems. Letting go of that — learning to ask questions instead of giving answers, to measure my success by my team's growth rather than my own contributions — has been one of the most difficult and rewarding things I've done.

I'm still learning. I don't think that part ever stops.

Working with AI

I also serve as an AI Ambassador in my organization, which sounds more official than it feels. In practice, it means I spend a lot of time helping engineering teams figure out where AI tools genuinely help and where they just add noise.

I'm not interested in the hype. I've sat through enough demos to know that the gap between "look what this can do" and "this actually improved our workflow" is enormous. What interests me is the unglamorous work of integration — taking AI capabilities and fitting them into the messy reality of how teams actually build software, day after day.

Some of what I've seen has been genuinely useful. Some of it has been a waste of time. The most valuable thing about the whole experience has been the conversations it's forced us to have about what good engineering actually looks like — conversations we should have been having all along, with or without AI.

Other things

Outside of work, I'm drawn to things that teach me how to think differently.

Game development interests me not because I'm trying to ship a game, but because game systems are one of the richest ways to study how complex systems behave. Incentive structures, emergent behavior, balancing competing interests, understanding how humans interact with designed systems — games are a laboratory for all of it.

Photography is how I practice slowing down. It forces me to notice details I'd otherwise walk past — the way light falls on a building, the expression someone has for half a second before they notice you're looking. In a career that rewards speed, having something that rewards patience feels necessary.

Japan has been a long-standing interest. The language, the culture, the idea of kaizen — continuous, incremental improvement — and the craftsmanship tradition of getting a little better at something every day for years. I'm currently learning Japanese, slowly and stubbornly, mostly through Anki cards and a refusal to give up.

Bass guitar is the newest addition. I'm bad at it. That's the point. Learning something you're bad at, as an adult, is a useful exercise in humility and a reminder that progress is almost always slower than you want it to be.

Why I write

This is the section I care about most, because it's the reason this site exists.

I write to think. Not to build an audience, not to establish thought leadership, not to optimize for engagement or SEO or any of the other things the internet tells you writing is for. I write because it's the most reliable way I've found to figure out what I actually believe.

There's a difference between having an opinion and having a thought. Opinions are easy — you can collect them passively, absorb them from conversations and articles and tweets, and repeat them without ever examining them closely. Thoughts are harder. They require you to sit with an idea, turn it over, poke at the weak spots, and be honest about the parts you're not sure about.

Writing forces that process. When I try to write down what I think about leadership, or AI adoption, or system design, I inevitably discover gaps — places where I was repeating something I'd heard rather than something I'd actually worked out for myself. The essay becomes the thinking, not just the record of it.

So this site is a public notebook. Some of what's here is polished. Some of it is me working things out in real time. If something here is useful to you, that's a genuine bonus. But the primary audience is me, six months from now, trying to remember what I was thinking and why.
